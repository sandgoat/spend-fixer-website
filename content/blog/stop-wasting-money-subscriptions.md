The average American spends $219/month on subscriptions — and underestimates that number by about 2.5x. That's not a budgeting problem. That's a visibility problem.

Here's how to find every subscription draining your account and actually do something about them.

## Why Subscriptions Are So Hard to Track

Free trials convert to paid silently. Annual renewals happen once a year so you forget they're coming. Prices creep up 20-30% with barely a notification. And you're probably splitting subscriptions across 3-4 different credit cards, making the full picture impossible to see.

## Step 1: Pull Every Transaction From the Last 12 Months

Don't rely on memory. Go back a full year, because annual subscriptions only hit once.

Look for: recurring amounts on the same date each month, small charges ($5-$20) you don't recognize, anything with "trial," "premium," or "plus" in the name.

## Step 2: Categorize Ruthlessly

For each subscription, ask: *Did I use this in the last 30 days?* Not "do I intend to use it" — did I actually use it.

If the answer is no, cancel it. You can always re-subscribe.

## Common Subscriptions People Forget

- Old streaming services from a free trial
- Gym memberships after moving
- Cloud storage from a phone you no longer own  
- Software subscriptions from a past job or project
- Magazine subscriptions that auto-renewed
- Domain/hosting renewals for abandoned projects

## The Numbers

If you cancel just 3 unused $10-$15/month subscriptions, that's $360-$540/year back in your pocket. For most people, the savings are much higher.

The hardest part isn't canceling — it's finding them all. That's where automation pays for itself.
