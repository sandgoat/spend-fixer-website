# Best Free Budgeting Apps in 2026 (Tested)

Mint is gone. You already know that. What you might not know is how dramatically the budgeting app landscape shifted in the two years since Intuit pulled the plug. Apps that were niche tools for personal finance nerds are now mainstream products with millions of users — and price tags to match.

This post covers the best budgeting apps available right now, what each one actually costs (in money and time), and who each one is built for. We tested all of them. No fluff.

---

## Why This List Matters More Than Ever

When Mint shut down in January 2024, roughly 3.6 million active users needed somewhere to go. Most migrated to Credit Karma (also Intuit), which doesn't really budget — it monitors credit. Others went to YNAB, Monarch Money, Copilot, or Rocket Money. Some just gave up.

The problem: most "free" alternatives aren't actually free. Or they're free but missing the core features that made Mint useful. This guide cuts through the noise.

---

## The Apps We Tested

### 1. YNAB (You Need a Budget)

**Cost:** $109/year (or $14.99/month)  
**Free tier:** 34-day trial only  
**Platforms:** Web, iOS, Android

YNAB is the gold standard for intentional budgeting. It uses zero-based budgeting — every dollar gets assigned a job before you spend it. The app is powerful, the community is passionate, and the methodology genuinely works if you commit to it.

**What's great:** Forces you to think proactively about money. Excellent bank sync. Strong reports. The philosophy actually changes behavior.

**What's not:** It has a real learning curve. Expect 2–3 hours to set it up properly and a week or two before it clicks. At $109/year, it's not cheap. And if you fall off the wagon for two weeks, coming back feels like restarting from scratch.

**Bottom line:** Best for people who want a system and are willing to invest time in it. Not for casual budgeters.

---

### 2. Monarch Money

**Cost:** $99.99/year (or $14.99/month)  
**Free tier:** 7-day trial  
**Platforms:** Web, iOS, Android

Monarch was built by Mint veterans and it shows. The interface is clean, the net worth tracking is excellent, and it has one of the best collaborative features for couples. It's become the go-to "Mint replacement" for power users.

**What's great:** Net worth dashboard, investment tracking, bill tracking, cash flow reports, and household sharing. Very polished.

**What's not:** At $99/year, you're paying nearly as much as YNAB without the budgeting methodology. It's more of a financial dashboard than a budgeting tool. Some users report bank connection issues.

**Bottom line:** Great if you want comprehensive financial visibility, especially for couples or people who track investments. Expensive for simple budgeting.

---

### 3. Copilot

**Cost:** $95.99/year (or $13/month)  
**Free tier:** 2-month free trial  
**Platforms:** iOS only (macOS in beta)

Copilot is the prettiest budgeting app on this list, full stop. It uses AI to auto-categorize transactions and learns your patterns over time. The design team clearly cared deeply about the experience.

**What's great:** Gorgeous UI, smart auto-categorization, subscription tracking, spending trends. The AI stuff actually works.

**What's not:** iOS only is a dealbreaker for Android users. Pricey for what you get. Some users find the onboarding overwhelming.

**Bottom line:** iPhone users who care about design and don't mind paying will love it. Everyone else should look elsewhere.

---

### 4. Rocket Money

**Cost:** Free tier available; Premium $6–$12/month  
**Free tier:** Yes, with limited features  
**Platforms:** Web, iOS, Android

Rocket Money (formerly Truebill) carved out a niche around subscription cancellation. They'll actually negotiate and cancel subscriptions on your behalf. The free tier is functional but stripped down.

**What's great:** Subscription tracking is genuinely excellent. The negotiation service has saved some users hundreds per year. Free tier exists.

**What's not:** The free tier is pretty bare — no budgeting, just tracking. Premium pricing is aggressive and somewhat opaque. Feels more like a financial services company than a budgeting tool.

**Bottom line:** Useful if you have subscription creep problems. Weaker as a daily budgeting tool.

---

### 5. NerdWallet

**Cost:** Free  
**Free tier:** Fully free  
**Platforms:** Web, iOS, Android

NerdWallet is truly free because it makes money recommending financial products. The budgeting features are basic — transaction tracking, spending categories, net worth overview. It works.

**What's great:** Actually free. Decent transaction sync. Good credit score monitoring.

**What's not:** The product recommendations can feel intrusive. The budgeting tools are surface-level. It's more of a financial health overview than a real budget.

**Bottom line:** Fine if you want something free with zero commitment. Not for serious budgeting.
