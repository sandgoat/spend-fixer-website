# What Happened to Mint? Best Alternatives in 2026

Mint — the free budgeting app that launched in 2006 and changed personal finance forever — shut down in January 2024. If you're still looking for a replacement, you're not alone. Millions of users were left scrambling, and two years later, a lot of people still haven't found a tool they actually like.

This post covers what happened, what Mint users lost, and the best alternatives right now.

---

## What Happened to Mint

Intuit acquired Mint in 2009 for $170 million. At the time, it was a smart move: Mint had popularized automated expense tracking and was growing fast. Intuit's plan was to integrate it with their broader financial products — TurboTax, QuickBooks, Credit Karma.

For years, Mint was the dominant free budgeting app. At its peak it had over 20 million registered users. But the cracks started showing around 2019–2020. Bank connection issues became chronic. The app felt increasingly neglected. Features barely changed for years.

Behind the scenes, Intuit had a problem. Mint was free, which meant Intuit made money off it by selling financial product recommendations — credit cards, loans, savings accounts. But Credit Karma, which they acquired in 2020 for $7.1 billion, did the same thing far more profitably. Mint and Credit Karma were cannibalizing each other's business model.

In November 2023, Intuit announced Mint would shut down January 1, 2024. Users could migrate to Credit Karma, which... isn't a budgeting app. It's a credit monitoring tool with some spending tracking bolted on. It's not the same thing.

The migration path satisfied almost no one.

---

## What Mint Users Actually Lost

If you used Mint seriously, here's what disappeared:

**Transaction history.** Years of categorized transactions — gone. Intuit offered a CSV export, but re-importing historical data into any new app is painful.

**Budgets.** All your configured budget categories, limits, and rules had to be rebuilt from scratch in a new app.

**Bill reminders.** Mint had solid bill tracking and payment reminders. Most alternatives don't replicate this well.

**Free comprehensive tracking.** This is the big one. Mint was genuinely free and genuinely useful. No equivalent free replacement exists that matches its full feature set.

**The habit.** For millions of people, Mint was their entire relationship with budgeting. When it died, they lost the habit entirely.

## The Best Mint Alternatives in 2026

Two years in, the dust has settled. Here are the real options:

---

### YNAB (You Need a Budget)

**Cost:** $109/year  
**Who it's for:** People who want to go deep on budgeting

YNAB is the most popular Mint alternative among serious budgeters. It uses zero-based budgeting — you assign every dollar a purpose before you spend it. It's genuinely transformative if you commit to it.

The catch: it costs $109/year and has a real learning curve. If Mint felt like too much work at times, YNAB might feel like even more work at first. That said, many former Mint users report that YNAB actually changed their financial habits in a way Mint never did.

Best for: People who bounced off Mint because it wasn't structured enough, not people who want something simpler.

---

### Monarch Money

**Cost:** $99.99/year  
**Who it's for:** Power users and couples

Monarch Money was literally built by ex-Mint employees who saw the writing on the wall. It has the cleanest financial dashboard in the space: net worth tracking, spending reports, investment overview, and excellent household sharing for couples.

If Mint's interface resonated with you — the overview, the category breakdown, the connected accounts — Monarch is probably your closest match. It's more polished than Mint ever was in its final years.

The catch: it's not free. At $100/year, it's a real commitment.

Best for: Households that want a comprehensive financial picture and don't mind paying for quality.

---

### Copilot

**Cost:** $95.99/year  
**Who it's for:** iPhone users

Copilot uses AI to categorize your spending and learns your patterns over time. The design is exceptional — probably the best-looking budgeting app available. It launched as an iOS-only product and has been expanding slowly.

If you're on Android, this isn't an option yet. If you're on iPhone and care about the quality of the experience, it's worth the free trial.

Best for: iPhone-only households who want smart automation and great design.

---

### Rocket Money

**Cost:** Free (limited) / $6–$12/month  
**Who it's for:** Subscription tracking

Rocket Money's standout feature is subscription cancellation. They'll actually negotiate with companies and cancel subscriptions on your behalf. As subscription costs have exploded over the past few years, this has become genuinely valuable.

As a Mint replacement for general budgeting, it's weaker. The free tier is functional but stripped down. The paid tier is useful if you have subscription sprawl.

Best for: Anyone who suspects they're paying for things they forgot about.

---

### NerdWallet

**Cost:** Free  
**Who it's for:** Passive trackers

NerdWallet's budgeting features are basic, but they're free. Connect your accounts, see your spending by category, track your net worth. It works. The trade-off is that NerdWallet makes money by recommending financial products, so you'll see those suggestions throughout.

If you want something that costs nothing and doesn't require much setup, NerdWallet is legitimate.

Best for: People who want free and don't need deep budgeting features.

## The Real Lesson From Mint's Death

Mint proved that millions of people want help managing their money — they just don't want it to feel like a second job. The apps that have thrived since Mint's shutdown are the ones that give something meaningful back for the time investment.

The apps that struggle are the ones that demand daily categorization, weekly budget reviews, and monthly reconciliations from people who can barely remember their account passwords.

If you've cycled through three or four Mint alternatives and none of them stuck, it might not be a you problem. It might be a design problem. Worth trying something built differently.
