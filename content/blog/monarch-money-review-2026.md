# Monarch Money Review 2026: Pros, Cons, and Alternatives

When Mint shut down in January 2024, Monarch Money was the most recommended replacement. It had better design, more features, and a business model that actually made sense (subscription, not ad-supported). Two years later, it's grown significantly and is genuinely one of the best full-featured personal finance tools available.

It's also $99/year, fairly complex, and built for a specific type of user. Whether it's right for you depends a lot on what you actually want from a budgeting app.

This is an honest review. We'll cover what Monarch does well, where it falls short, and who should look elsewhere.

---

## What Is Monarch Money?

Monarch Money is a personal finance app that launched in 2021 and gained massive traction after Mint's shutdown. It connects to your bank accounts, credit cards, investment accounts, and loans — giving you a full picture of your financial life in one place.

The core features:

- **Account aggregation** — connects to thousands of banks, brokerages, and credit unions
- **Budget tracking** — category-based budgets with rollover support
- **Net worth tracking** — all your assets and liabilities in one dashboard
- **Cash flow analysis** — income vs. expenses over time
- **Goals** — savings goals with progress tracking
- **Collaboration** — multiple users can share a household account
- **Transaction management** — auto-categorization, manual overrides, splits, notes

It's a comprehensive tool. If you want everything in one place and you're willing to pay for it, Monarch delivers.

**Current pricing:** $99.99/year or $14.99/month. No free tier — there's a 30-day free trial.

## What Monarch Does Well

### Net Worth Dashboard

This is genuinely excellent. Monarch connects to investment accounts (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab, etc.) alongside your checking and savings accounts, giving you a real-time net worth number that updates as markets move. For people who want a single view of their complete financial picture — not just day-to-day spending — this is hard to beat.

The net worth graph over time is motivating in a way that a plain budget isn't. Watching that number trend upward because of both savings behavior and investment returns gives you a broader sense of financial progress.

### Household Collaboration

Monarch lets two users share one account, each with their own login. This is genuinely useful for couples or anyone managing finances jointly. You can see each other's transactions, work from the same budget, and discuss spending without having to screenshot and text pictures of spreadsheets to each other.

Most budgeting apps handle this poorly. Monarch handles it well.

### Budget Flexibility

Monarch's budget system is more flexible than most. You can set monthly budgets by category, enable rollover (so unused funds carry forward), create one-time budget events, and group categories into custom sections. It's more configuration than YNAB but more flexible than simpler tools.

The rollover feature is particularly useful for irregular spending categories like clothing or car maintenance — you accumulate a buffer in months you don't spend, then draw it down when you do.

### Design and UX

Monarch's design is clean. It's one of the better-looking personal finance apps, which matters more than it should because you actually have to look at it. The mobile app is solid, the web app is feature-complete, and the navigation makes sense.

### Customer Support

Compared to Mint (which had notoriously bad support) and YNAB (which is mostly self-serve documentation), Monarch has responsive customer support. Email response times are generally fast, and they actively communicate about product changes.

## Where Monarch Falls Short

### Price

$99.99/year is real money. There are capable free alternatives and cheaper paid options. For users who just need basic budgeting — track spending, stay within a monthly target — paying $100/year for advanced features they'll never use is hard to justify.

If you use the net worth tracking, investment integration, and collaboration features, the price makes more sense. If you're using Monarch as a slightly nicer Mint replacement for basic spend tracking, there are cheaper ways to do that.

### No Free Tier

The 30-day trial is generous, but the complete lack of a free tier means there's no low-stakes way to see if Monarch fits into your life. For people who have churned through several budgeting apps already, committing $100 before they know if they'll stick with it is a barrier.

### Complexity for Simple Needs

Monarch is feature-rich, which is great until you just want to know if you're overspending this month. The dashboard surfaces a lot of information, and for some users, more information creates more confusion rather than clarity.

If you don't need net worth tracking, investment integration, or collaborative household features, Monarch's complexity is overhead with no benefit. You're paying for features you're not using and navigating around them to find the thing you actually care about.

### Transaction Sync Can Be Unreliable

Like all apps that depend on bank connections (through Plaid or MX), Monarch occasionally has sync issues. Some smaller credit unions or regional banks have unreliable connections. This has gotten better over two years of development but it's still not perfect. When transactions don't sync, your budget numbers are wrong and you don't always get notified.

### Mobile App vs. Web App Gap

The mobile app is good but the web app is where Monarch shines. Complex budgeting tasks (setting up categories, analyzing trends, managing goals) are easier on desktop. If you're exclusively a mobile user, you might find the experience a bit awkward.

## Who Should Use Monarch Money

Monarch is a strong fit if:

- **You want a complete financial picture.** Net worth, investments, debts, and daily spending all in one place. If that integration matters to you, Monarch does it better than almost anything.
- **You're managing finances with a partner.** The collaboration feature is genuinely useful and few competitors do it as well.
- **You came from Mint and want a like-for-like replacement.** Monarch has most of what Mint had plus more. If you were a power Mint user, Monarch will feel familiar and more capable.
- **You're comfortable paying for software.** At $100/year, you need to be okay with that spend. People who resent paying for budgeting apps usually don't stick with Monarch.
- **You enjoy engaging with your finances in detail.** If weekly budget reviews and trend analysis are interesting to you rather than tedious, Monarch's depth is rewarding.

---

## Who Should Look Elsewhere

Monarch may not be right if:

- **You just need to stop overspending.** If your financial situation is "I make decent money but somehow always run out," you don't need net worth tracking and investment dashboards. You need a simple spending check.
- **Budget software hasn't worked for you before.** Adding more features and a higher price tag rarely fixes the fundamental problem of not wanting to look at the numbers. A simpler tool with less friction might be more effective.
- **You want free or very cheap.** There are capable free tools. Monarch doesn't have a free option.
- **You want something faster.** Monarch requires some upkeep. If you want a 60-second daily check-in, Monarch is overkill.

## Monarch Money Alternatives

### YNAB ($109/year)
More expensive than Monarch, deeper budgeting methodology. Better for zero-based budgeting enthusiasts, debt payoff, and variable income. Less good for net worth tracking and couple collaboration. [Read our YNAB comparison →](/blog/ynab-vs-spendfixer)

### Copilot ($95/year, iOS only)
Beautiful design, smart AI categorization, Apple ecosystem integration. The best-looking budgeting app available. The dealbreaker: iPhone and iPad only. No Android, no web app.

### Rocket Money (free tier, $6–12/month for premium)
Strong subscription management and bill negotiation features. The free tier is surprisingly capable for basic tracking. Better for people whose main problem is forgotten subscriptions and unnecessary recurring charges. Less good for comprehensive budgeting.

### NerdWallet (free)
Free, connects to accounts, shows net worth and credit score. Not really a budgeting tool — more of a financial overview plus credit monitoring. Fine as a free check-in but limited for actual budget management.

## Monarch Money in 2026: The Verdict

Monarch Money is genuinely good. The product has matured, the design is polished, and the feature set is comprehensive. For users who want a serious full-featured personal finance tool and are willing to pay for it, Monarch is arguably the best option in the market right now.

The limitations are real though: $99.99/year with no free tier, meaningful complexity for users with simple needs, and a learning curve that will deter people who just want to see if their spending is on track.

**Rating: 4.2/5** — excellent for power users, overkill for everyone else.

If you're a detail-oriented person who manages finances actively and wants everything in one place, Monarch is likely worth it. If you've bounced off budgeting apps before and want something you'll actually use every day, a simpler tool might serve you better.

The best personal finance app is the one you check every day. Make your choice accordingly.
